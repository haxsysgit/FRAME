# Frame Lab Fixture Selection Principles

This note corrects a bad instinct:
selecting fixtures by size, fame, or ecosystem prestige.

That is not the goal.

## Real goal

Frame Lab exists to fit and score candidate FRAME/schema structures across different real projects until we find the most adoptable and stable structure.

That means the fixture set should optimize for:
- meaningful structural variety
- real projectness
- fast repeated fitting
- easy comparison across subagents
- low friction when extracting important details first, then iterating on fit and scoring

## What a good fixture is

A good Frame Lab fixture is:
- real, not fictional
- structured enough to reveal actual project reality
- small or medium enough for fast agent passes
- different enough from other fixtures to expose weak assumptions
- stable enough that repeated scoring is not chaos

## What a bad fixture is

A bad fixture is:
- huge just because it is famous
- so noisy that fitting becomes archaeology instead of evaluation
- too template-like to expose real-world structure
- too weird to be representative unless it is deliberately chosen as an edge-case fixture

## Best working balance

The best default fixture set is not the biggest or most complete set.
It is the smallest assorted set that still creates real schema pressure.

That usually means:
- real repos
- small-to-medium size
- clear project identity
- diverse enough surfaces
- limited count for fast repeated scoring

## Practical recommendation

For early multi-agent fitting, prefer a compact lab set such as:
- 1 CLI tool
- 1 web product
- 1 API/devtool product
- 1 desktop or mobile app
- 1 data-ish or infra-ish system
- 1 AI-native system
- 1 legacy or mixed-shape repo

That is enough to create meaningful pressure without slowing the loop too much.

## Evaluation workflow assumption

The intended workflow is:
1. extract the important project details first
2. have multiple subagents fit candidate schemas/FRAME structures
3. score each fit for coverage, clarity, stability, and bloat
4. compare across fixtures
5. adjust the architecture or schema
6. repeat until the sweet spot becomes clear

That means fixture selection should serve iteration speed and signal quality.
Not repo size.

## Selection rule

When choosing between two candidate fixtures from the same broad family, prefer the one that is:
- more real than template-like
- more structured than chaotic
- lighter to inspect
- easier to compare repeatedly

unless the heavier repo reveals a unique pressure that smaller repos cannot.

## Bottom line

Frame Lab should use real and structured-enough repos, not giant repos by default.
The fixtures are there to make fitting and scoring sharper, faster, and more honest.
